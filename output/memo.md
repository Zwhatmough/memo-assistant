## 1. Executive Summary

Auto Trader operates the UK's largest automotive marketplace, connecting vehicle buyers and sellers through a digital platform that dominates category time-on-site [E-002, E-003]. In FY2026, Group revenue reached £624.3m and Group operating profit grew to £392.7m, representing a 63.0% operating margin [E-034, E-035, E-036]. The core Autotrader marketplace delivered a materially higher 70.0% operating margin, underscoring the asset-light economics of the digital platform [E-052]. Basic earnings per share stood at 34.17 pence [E-037]. The investment thesis rests on Auto Trader's durable scale advantage, strong cash conversion supporting a long track record of cash generation, and an accelerated shareholder return programme including share buybacks [E-007, E-147, E-148]. Management has stated its belief that the prevailing share price does not reflect the Company's underlying value [E-149]. The principal risk relates to softening retailer sentiment tied to the Deal Builder product roll-out and broader cost pressures, alongside emerging technological disintermediation risks from agentic AI and new entrants such as Amazon Autos [E-122, E-131, E-132].

## 2. Business Overview

Auto Trader operates as a marketplace platform connecting buyers and sellers of vehicles, functioning as the UK's largest automotive marketplace and leading digital platform in the category [E-001, E-002]. The platform commands an outsized share of consumer attention, accounting for over 75% of all minutes spent on automotive classified sites and recording 81.7m monthly visits in 2026 [E-003, E-004]. The Group operates across three main segments: the core Autotrader marketplace, Trade, and Autorama [E-028]. Revenue generation is driven primarily by a multi-revenue retailer model encompassing Retailer, Home Trader, and Commercial products, with Average Revenue Per Retailer (ARPR) as the key monetisation metric, reaching £2,995 per month in FY2026 [E-012, E-018, E-056]. The average number of retailer forecourts using the platform was 13,942 in the period, having declined 0.5% year-on-year [E-016, E-017]. Autorama, the Group's vehicle-buying and retailing operation, delivered 8,056 vehicles in 2026 and generated £39.0m of revenue, though it remains a modest contributor to the overall Group [E-031, E-071]. Deal Builder has emerged as a central strategic product supporting the Group's digital retailing ambitions, scaling to 6.7k retailers by March 2026, up from 2.0k in March 2025, with 137,000 deals reaching full reservation status in the year [E-089, E-100, E-101]. The product is positioned to extend the car-buying journey further online, complementing other digital retailing capabilities and AI-enabled tools such as Co-Driver and Buying Signals [E-093, E-108, E-102]. Auto Trader's proprietary dataset is also integrated with over 220 technology partners, reflecting a broader ambition to expand technology and data services to finance, insurance, and manufacturer partners beyond the core marketplace [E-020, E-104].

## 3. Financial and Operating Performance

Group revenue increased by 4% year-on-year to £624.3m, with the FINANCIAL METRICS indicating growth of 3.9% (FY2025: £601.1m to FY2026: £624.3m) [E-034, E-041]. Core Autotrader revenue grew to £585.3m, equivalent to 3.6% growth over the prior year [E-050, E-055]. This growth was underpinned primarily by ARPR, which increased 4.9% (+£141) to £2,995, driven by product and price levers, with price contributing the larger share, partially offset by a negative contribution from declining retailer stock [E-018, E-067]. Group operating profit rose to £392.7m, a 4% increase as reported, consistent with the calculated 4.2% growth (FY2025: £376.8m) [E-042, E-035]. The Group operating profit margin was 63.0%, materially below the core Autotrader segment's 70.0% margin, reflecting the dilutive effect of the lower-margin Autorama and Trade segments [E-036, E-052, E-060]. Group profit before tax was £388.8m, and profit for the year totalled £293.9m, with an effective tax rate of 24.0% [E-061, E-048, E-080]. Basic earnings per share were 34.17 pence, with diluted EPS of 34.07 pence [E-037, E-081].

Cash generation remained a defining feature of the business, with cash generated from operations of £418.0m, up 4.6% year-on-year (FY2025: £399.7m), and net cash generated from operating activities of £322.8m [E-058, E-063]. The FINANCIAL METRICS indicate a cash conversion rate of 106.4% against operating profit, consistent with an asset-light digital platform model. The balance sheet remained conservatively positioned, with net bank debt of £146.8m and leverage of 0.3 times net debt/EBITDA as at 31 March 2026, alongside borrowings of £163.4m and total equity of £407.1m [E-064, E-065, E-084, E-068]. Capital returns to shareholders were substantial, totalling £463.2m in the year, comprising £369.1m of share buyback consideration and £94.1m of dividends paid, with 58.5m shares purchased and cancelled under the buyback programme [E-146, E-151, E-157, E-154]. Over the three years to 2026, total returns to shareholders reached £989.2m [E-155]. Total dividend per share for the year was 11.6 pence [E-053, E-066]. For FY2027, Group operating profit is guided to £395m–£415m, with management targeting a further £600m of shareholder returns [E-039, E-150].

## 4. Value Drivers

- ARPR growth of 4.9% (+£141), driven primarily by pricing and product levers, more than offset a negative contribution from declining retailer stock, making it the single largest driver of core revenue growth in the period [E-067].
- The scaling of Deal Builder from 2.0k to 6.7k retailers, with full-reservation deals growing from 49k to 137k, represents a monetisable digital retailing product that could support future ARPR and engagement despite near-term adoption friction [E-089].
- AI-enabled products, including Co-Driver and Buying Signals, are generating measurable engagement and efficiency gains that could underpin future retailer willingness to pay and reduce churn [E-088, E-102].
- Autorama's transition to a single reporting segment from FY2027, combined with an expected move to a small profit, could reduce the current modest margin drag on the Group [E-096, E-072].
- Expansion of data and technology services to finance, insurance, and manufacturer partners via API integrations represents an underdeveloped monetisation avenue leveraging the Group's proprietary dataset [E-093, E-020, E-104].

## 5. Strengths

- Auto Trader holds a dominant, defensible market position, accounting for over 75% of all minutes spent on automotive classified sites and being 11x larger than its nearest competitor by time on site [E-003, E-007].
- The core Autotrader marketplace generates a very high operating margin of 70.0%, reflecting the asset-light, network-effect-driven nature of the digital marketplace business model [E-052].
- Cash generation is exceptionally strong, with cash from operations growing 4.6% year-on-year to £418.0m, supporting the Group's stated long track record of strong cash generation [E-062, E-147].
- The balance sheet is conservatively levered at 0.3x net debt/EBITDA, providing financial flexibility to fund growth investment and shareholder returns [E-065].
- ARPR growth of 4.9% to £2,995 demonstrates continued pricing power even as forecourt numbers softened, indicating resilient monetisation of the existing retailer base [E-018].

## 6. Material Risks

### Disclosed risks

Retailer sentiment softened during the year due to difficult cost-related trading conditions and concerns with the speed and nature of the Deal Builder product roll-out, contributing to a slight decrease in retailer numbers [E-122]. Employee engagement fell significantly from 91% to 72%, attributed to restructures, a tighter office-working approach, and negative sentiment tied to Deal Builder scaling and share price weakness [E-117, E-118]. Consumer behaviour is shifting toward conversational AI/LLM interfaces, which could reduce reliance on traditional search and marketplace functionality over time [E-121]. The emergence of agentic AI presents disintermediation risk, potentially directing consumers directly to listings and reducing retailers' need to purchase prominence on the marketplace [E-131]. Structural supply headwinds exist as 2.5 million "lost" pandemic-era registrations age through the vehicle parc, forecasting reduced availability of used vehicles that could constrain retailer stock and activity [E-123].

### Inferred risks

The rapid pace of the share buyback programme, with 58.5 million shares purchased in FY26 and plans to increase leverage toward approximately 1.0x in FY27, may suggest that capital return is becoming increasingly concentrated in equity repurchases, which could reduce balance sheet flexibility relative to the current near-zero net debt position [E-148, E-150, E-065]. It appears that revenue growth is increasingly reliant on price and product levers rather than volume, which may imply diminishing returns or heightened retailer resistance if pricing actions are pushed further, as suggested by the November 2025 feedback backlash [E-067, E-124].

### Items requiring further investigation

The available evidence does not clarify what specific product or process changes management has implemented in response to the November 2025 Deal Builder feedback, nor what leading indicators would demonstrate whether retailer sentiment has stabilised [E-124, E-099]. The available evidence does not clarify the expected timeline or potential financial and reputational exposure arising from the CMA's investigation into online reviews involving Autotrader [E-137].

## 7. Bull and Bear Cases

### Bull case

Auto Trader's scale advantage—11x larger than its nearest competitor and commanding over 75% of category time-on-site—may suggest a durable moat that is difficult for new entrants, including Amazon Autos, to replicate in the near term [E-003, E-007].

Management's stated belief that the share price does not reflect underlying value, combined with an accelerated buyback programme and expected FY2027 operating profit of £395m–£415m, points to a business generating substantial capital return capacity [E-149, E-148, E-039].

High cash conversion of 106.4% and low leverage of 0.3x net debt/EBITDA provide flexibility to fund growth investment, buybacks, and dividends concurrently [E-065].

Early traction in AI-driven products such as Co-Driver and Buying Signals, which reduced average listing time from 28 minutes to 5 minutes, may position the platform to embed itself deeper into retailer workflows against disintermediation risk [E-106, E-102].

### Bear case

Retailer forecourt numbers declined for a second consecutive period, with management directly linking this to pushback on Deal Builder execution and difficult trading conditions, contributing a negative stock impact to ARPR growth [E-127, E-128].

Structural used car supply headwinds, driven by "lost" pandemic-era registrations, could constrain retailer inventory and Autotrader's addressable stock base over the medium term [E-123].

Revenue growth is increasingly dependent on pricing and product levers rather than retailer or stock volume growth, which may imply limited headroom if retailer resistance intensifies [E-067, E-124].

Plans to increase leverage toward approximately 1.0x net debt/EBITDA to fund an expanded shareholder return programme reduce financial headroom amid emerging competitive threats such as Amazon Autos and agentic AI [E-150, E-132, E-131].

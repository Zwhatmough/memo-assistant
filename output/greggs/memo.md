# Greggs plc (GRG) — Investment Memo

## 1. Executive Summary

Greggs is the UK's leading food-to-go brand, operating a vertically-integrated model across a 2,700-strong shop estate, franchise partnerships, grocery retail tie-ups and delivery channels [E-001, E-078]. In 2025, total sales rose to £2,151.0m from £2,014.0m in 2024, with company-managed like-for-like sales growth of 2.4% [E-034, E-049, E-035]. However, underlying pre-tax profit declined to £171.9m from £189.8m, and statutory profit before tax fell to £167.4m from £203.9m, reflecting cost inflation and investment costs [E-036, E-051, E-037, E-052]. The investment case rests on continued market share gains, a large unsaturated shop estate expansion runway, underpenetrated evening and delivery dayparts, and growing digital/app engagement, all underpinned by strong cash generation of £273.7m and a maintained total dividend of 69.0 pence per share [E-004, E-088, E-028, E-066, E-164]. The principal risk to this thesis is a multi-year period of margin and ROCE compression — ROCE fell to 16.0% in 2025 with management guiding to further declines before stabilising in 2027 — driven by the capital-intensive Derby and Kettering distribution centre investment cycle [E-163, E-077, E-122].

## 2. Business Overview

Greggs is a UK-based food-to-go retailer whose purpose is to make great tasting, freshly prepared food and drink accessible, operating through a 2,700-strong shop estate at year-end 2025, supplemented by franchise partners, business-to-business relationships, grocery retail tie-ups, and delivery [E-002, E-001, E-029, E-097]. The business is vertically integrated, with its own manufacturing and logistics network underpinning cost competitiveness and a No.1 value ranking in the YouGov BrandIndex within the quick-service sector [E-078, E-017]. Revenue is earned primarily through company-managed shop sales, supplemented by franchised unit system sales (which grew 4.3% like-for-like in 2025) and business-to-business and grocery channels, including relationships with Iceland Foods and Tesco for delivered goods [E-050, E-032]. Products are now available in 800 larger Tesco stores and online, extending reach beyond the core estate [E-085].

Operationally, the company opened 121 net new shops in 2025, reaching its 2,700th shop in November, with 53% of new openings (excluding relocations) in areas without an existing Greggs within a mile, indicating continued white space for expansion [E-005, E-021, E-107]. Home delivery, offered from more than 1,600 shops via Just Eat and Uber Eats, represented 6.8% of company-managed sales, while the Greggs App accompanied 26.7% of company-managed transactions, used by over 1.5 million customers a week at peak in December [E-026, E-012, E-016, E-083]. Evening trading remains a developing daypart, with evening sales at 9.4% of company-managed food sales and a 2.9% share of evening visits [E-028, E-027]. The Group employs more than 33,000 colleagues and reported a colleague engagement score of 72.0 percent in 2025 [E-018, E-156]. Greggs was ranked the UK's No.1 considered food-to-go brand for the second year running and No.1 for value since 2010, with 1 in 3 GB adults having eaten at Greggs during the year [E-023, E-024, E-009].

## 3. Financial and Operating Performance

Total sales grew to £2,151.0m in 2025 from £2,014.0m in 2024, with revenue reported at £2,151.2m for the 52 weeks ended 27 December 2025 [E-034, E-049, E-043]. Company-managed like-for-like sales growth was 2.4%, while franchised units delivered like-for-like system sales growth of 4.3% [E-035, E-050]. Underlying operating profit was £187.5m, with an underlying operating profit margin of 8.7%, and underlying operating profit declined by 4.0% year-on-year [E-044, E-059, E-053]. Underlying pre-tax profit fell to £171.9m from £189.8m in 2024, while statutory profit before tax declined to £167.4m from £203.9m [E-036, E-051, E-037, E-052]. Profit after tax was £122.2m [E-060].

Margin pressure was driven by like-for-like cost inflation of around 5.5% in 2025, partially offset by £13.0m of structural cost savings delivered during the year [E-064, E-128, E-054, E-063]. Underlying diluted earnings per share were 122.8 pence, while diluted EPS including exceptional items was 119.3 pence, down from prior-year levels implied by the fall in statutory profit before tax [E-041, E-046, E-058, E-061].

Cash generation remained robust: net cash inflow from operating activities after lease payments was £273.7m, equating to diluted operating cash inflow per share of 267.1 pence [E-042, E-066, E-070]. Year-end net cash and cash equivalents stood at £45.8m, with total available liquidity of £145.8m including a £100.0m RCF facility [E-071, E-072, E-073]. Capital expenditure was elevated at £287.5m in 2025, with 2026 guidance of £200.0m as investment in new National Distribution Centres in Derby and Kettering continues [E-065, E-091, E-076].

Return on capital employed (ROCE) declined to 16.0% in 2025, and management has guided that ROCE will remain under pressure before stabilising in 2027 and recovering thereafter, reflecting the short-term capital intensity of the investment programme [E-048, E-163, E-077, E-121]. The Board recommended maintaining the total ordinary dividend at 69.0 pence per share, comprising an interim dividend of 19.0 pence and a final dividend of 50.0 pence [E-164, E-165, E-068, E-069]. Early trading in the first 9 weeks of 2026 showed total sales growth of 6.3% and like-for-like sales growth of 1.6%, though management expects 2026 profit before tax to be broadly in line with 2025 [E-056, E-055, E-038].

## 4. Value Drivers

- Continued shop estate expansion — 121 net new shops in 2025, targeting approximately 120 in 2026 — with over half of new openings in areas without an existing Greggs within a mile, points to substantial unsaturated white space for organic unit growth [E-005, E-106, E-107].
- Evening daypart and delivery remain underpenetrated relative to potential, with evening sales at 9.4% of company-managed food sales and delivery at 6.8%, representing incremental growth levers as Greggs closes the gap to best-in-class competitors [E-028, E-015].
- Investment in new automated National Distribution Centres in Derby and Kettering is designed to support scaling toward a larger shop estate with improved efficiency, positioning the business for margin recovery once the capital expenditure cycle completes [E-086, E-092, E-076].
- Grocery retail partnership expansion, including the Tesco rollout to 800 larger stores and online, extends Greggs' reach beyond its own estate at relatively low incremental capital cost [E-085].
- Growing App engagement, at 26.7% of company-managed transactions and 1.5 million weekly peak users, may support a lower-cost direct customer engagement channel, though the filings do not disclose paid versus direct acquisition mix, so any cost advantage cannot be fully verified [E-013, E-083].

## 5. Strengths

- Greggs is the UK's leading food-to-go brand by YouGov BrandIndex, ranked No.1 for value since 2010 and No.1 considered food-to-go brand for the second year running, with 1 in 3 GB adults having eaten at Greggs in 2025 [E-008, E-009, E-023, E-024].
- The company grew its share of food-to-go visits by 0.5 percentage points, with gains across dayparts, demonstrating outperformance in a tough consumer market [E-004, E-150].
- A vertically-integrated supply chain and structural cost savings of £13.0m delivered in 2025 provide a durable cost advantage supporting the company's value leadership positioning [E-078, E-054, E-120].
- Digital engagement is scaling, with App usage reaching over 1.5 million weekly users at peak and accompanying 26.7% of company-managed transactions, giving Greggs a growing direct, loyalty-linked customer base [E-083, E-016, E-013].
- Strong cash generation of £273.7m in net operating cash after lease payments and £145.8m of total available liquidity support continued investment and shareholder returns during the current margin-pressured investment phase [E-066, E-073].

## 6. Material Risks

### IT systems and cyber security
Cyber threats are intensifying and Greggs' reliance on business-critical technology creates exposure to extended disruption, data loss, litigation and fines; loss of key IT systems is explicitly identified as a source of potential operational disruption [E-130, E-134, E-135, E-139, E-140, E-147].

### Operational
Dependence on key suppliers and single production sites such as Balliol Park, alongside talent attraction and retention challenges and possible misalignment with franchise partners, could disrupt product availability, service standards and delivery of the wider growth strategy [E-138, E-142, E-147].

### Climate change and environmental obligations
Extreme weather, including the prolonged high temperatures that affected 2025 footfall, and new environmental regulation threaten operations and supply chain resilience, potentially requiring costly product or advertising changes, notwithstanding that this is not currently treated as a principal risk [E-132, E-137, E-145, E-127].

### Macroeconomic and geopolitical risk
Subdued consumer confidence and disposable income pressure are expected to persist into 2026, while global tensions and conflicts create unpredictable operating conditions, together threatening demand resilience and supply chain stability across the planning horizon [E-133, E-125, E-123].

### Brand and reputation
As Greggs' brand profile grows, so does its exposure to reputational damage from internal or external sources; misalignment with franchise, grocery or delivery partners could limit market access, and a brand-damaging food scare is modelled to cause a circa 25% sales reduction for six months [E-141, E-143, E-129].

### Financial
Greggs carries a principal risk around access to liquidity in the event of a sudden, material reduction in solvency; stress testing under combined scenarios showed a potential, though considered remote, breach of loan covenants in 2027, which could force delays to investment plans [E-136, E-146, E-149].

### Financial and market risk
Like-for-like cost inflation of around 5.5% in 2025, driven by employment costs including higher employer NICs plus food and packaging costs, compressed margins and profitability during the year, illustrating ongoing exposure to input cost volatility [E-128].

### Legal and regulatory compliance
The business faces the risk of producing or selling unsafe products due to incorrect allergen labelling, contamination or procedural non-compliance, which could cause customer or colleague harm, brand damage, sales impact and significant fines [E-144].

### Analytical observations
It appears that growing adoption of GLP-1 weight-loss drugs represents an emerging structural demand risk not yet reflected within Greggs' disclosed principal risk categories, as it may durably reduce demand for the calorie-dense bakery products central to the core range [E-124]. This may suggest that menu evolution toward higher-protein and alternative-diet products is, in part, a pre-emptive response to this trend, though the filings do not explicitly frame it this way [E-099, E-102].

### Items requiring further investigation
The specific mitigating actions and capital committed to cyber security, and whether any material cyber incidents have occurred to date, warrant further investigation given cyber risk is flagged repeatedly across the filing [E-130, E-135, E-140]. Additionally, the precise covenant terms, headroom and scenario assumptions underlying the 2027 stress-test breach finding are not detailed and would benefit from further disclosure [E-149].

## 7. Bull and Bear Cases

### Bull case
- Greggs continues to outperform a challenging food-to-go market, gaining 0.5pp of visit share to 8.6% while retaining its No.1 value ranking, suggesting resilient demand despite weak consumer spending [E-004, E-024, E-150].
- A substantial structural growth runway remains, with management targeting expansion from 2,700 to 3,500+ shops alongside underpenetrated evening and delivery dayparts and expanding grocery partnerships [E-014, E-108, E-085].
- Strong cash generation of £273.7m and a maintained total dividend of 69.0 pence provide downside protection and support shareholder returns even as ROCE normalises [E-066, E-165].
- Early 2026 trading, with total sales growth of 6.3% and like-for-like growth of 1.6% in the first 9 weeks, suggests the top-line growth algorithm remains intact [E-055, E-056].

### Bear case
- Underlying pre-tax profit fell from £189.8m in 2024 to £171.9m in 2025, with underlying operating profit down around 4%, evidencing margin compression from cost inflation and investment costs [E-036, E-051, E-053].
- ROCE declined to 16.0% in 2025 from 20.3% in 2024, with management guiding to further declines before stabilising in 2027, implying a multi-year period of depressed capital returns [E-122, E-094].
- Diluted EPS of 119.3p is closely tied to the fall in statutory profit before tax from £203.9m to £167.4m; it appears current EPS trends reflect weaker underlying earnings rather than operational strength, though evidence of any buyback-driven divergence is not available [E-061, E-037, E-052].
- 2026 profit before tax is expected to be only broadly in line with 2025, with improvement contingent on an uncertain consumer backdrop, capping near-term earnings visibility [E-038, E-154].

2. What is the expected trajectory and confidence level behind the ROCE recovery plan, stabilising in 2027 and recovering from 2028, and what milestones will management use to demonstrate progress before then [E-077, E-094].
3. What specific terms, headroom and scenario assumptions underlie the stress-tested potential breach of loan covenants in 2027, and what covenant flexibility currently exists under normal trading conditions [E-149].
4. What mitigating actions and capital investment are underway to address cyber security risk, and has Greggs experienced any material cyber incidents to date, given repeated flagging of this risk across the filing [E-130, E-135, E-140].
5. What is the split of App/website traffic between direct and paid acquisition channels, and how does this compare to peers, given the data needed to substantiate any customer-acquisition-cost advantage is not disclosed [E-013, E-083].
6. How is management quantifying and monitoring the potential structural demand impact from GLP-1 weight-loss drug adoption on core bakery categories, and is this shaping medium-term menu strategy, since this risk is not yet reflected in disclosed principal risk categories [E-124].
7. What contingency plans exist for potential delays or cost overruns at the Derby and Kettering distribution centres, given these are explicitly linked to elevated 2026 fixed costs and capital intensity [E-122, E-126, E-091].
